Research

"Staying in CTRL": Mapping the Impacts of AI on Journalism and Normative Strategies of AI Control

Ph.D. Dissertation · Defended 29 September 2025 · University of Southern Denmark
Supervisors: Morten Skovsgaard & Claes de Vreese

The dissertation takes AI as its object — conceptualizing it as a hyperobject: a phenomenon too large, distributed, and entangled to be grasped from any single vantage point. Because of this, it attacks the problem from three angles, moving through the normative (what ethical frameworks are we using?), the experiential (what do journalists actually go through?), and the discursive (how does language in law and policy shape what AI gets to do?) — arguing that meaningful control requires borrowing from fields usually kept apart: philosophy, organizational theory, medicine, and jurisprudence.

Papers

  • Paper I

    Striking the (im)balance Published

    Sigsgaard, M. E. (2024). Striking the (im)balance – a review of the relative prevalence of meta-ethical models in AI journalism research. Journalism. doi:10.1177/14648849241303718

    Abstract

    A systematic review of the ethical assumptions at work in AI journalism research. The paper finds that the field skews heavily toward negative-utilitarian approaches — focused on identifying and mitigating current harms — while deontological and virtue-ethical perspectives are rarely employed. The consequence is that first-order normative goals often go unstated, making it difficult to move from reactive harm-mitigation to proactive demands on AI developers. The paper argues that drawing on a wider range of ethical models would lead to better technology, better research, and better journalistic practice.

  • Paper II

    Preparing Journalism for Robots: AI Integration through Role Clarity In review

    Sigsgaard, M. (in review). Preparing Journalism for Robots: AI Integration through Role Clarity.

    Abstract

    Based on fifteen in-depth interviews with employees at TV2, TV2 Fyn, and Jysk Fynske Medier conducted in 2022 — the year ChatGPT launched — this paper examines how AI integration impacts role clarity in newsrooms. Borrowing a phenomenological-hermeneutic methodology from medical research on patients adapting to assistive technology, it traces how role ambiguity emerges from unclear hierarchical relationships to AI systems, improvised learning, and disconnects between stated AI strategies and lived experience. Role clarity — the degree to which job expectations are clearly understood — proves a more tractable and empirically useful lens for studying AI integration than the journalistic identity frameworks that typically dominate the field.

  • Paper III

    Moral "Actors" and "Responsible AI" Dissertation paper

    Sigsgaard, M. (2025). Moral "Actors" and "Responsible AI": How Linguistic Attribution of Human Abilities and Legal Rights to AI Creates a "Techno-Rights Gap". In Staying in CTRL. University of Southern Denmark.

    Abstract

    This paper examines how language in AI policy and law — "responsible AI," "trustworthy AI," AI that "reads" and "learns" — constitutes a series of category mistakes with practical legal consequences. When moral agency is attributed to automated systems, a responsibility gap emerges: something went wrong, it was caused by technology, there is a legitimate demand for accountability, but the technology is not a legitimate target. Meanwhile, AI companies use the same anthropomorphizing language to claim quasi-rights for their systems in copyright disputes, arguing that their models "learn" and are "inspired" just as humans are. The paper coins the term techno-rights gap to describe how AI can be granted rights-adjacent status while its owners evade corresponding obligations — and argues that legislators must pre-empt rather than react if democratic governance is to meaningfully shape AI development.
